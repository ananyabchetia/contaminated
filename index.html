A guilty plea was just the beginning: Inside a town's fight for a cleaner future

Video is a screen recording of JH Baxter site via Google Images

By Ananya Chetia

Oregon native Eric Dion still remembers the pungent odor stinging his neighborhood in the 1980s from the wood treatment facility across the street.

"I paid no attention to it," Eric said, who's now in treatment for Diffuse Leptomeningeal Glioneuronal Tumor, a rare slow growing brain tumor that mostly affects children. He and his wife Lisa are convinced living next to contaminated site JH Baxter led to Eric's cancer.

Located in Bethel neighborhood in West Eugene Oregon, the corporation pleaded guilty to four federal pollution charges and was ordered to pay $1.5 million in criminal fines in January. The president, 61-year-old Georgia Baxter-Krause from Deschutes, Oregon, pleaded guilty to two counts of making false statements to Oregon inspectors and will be sentenced on April 22, which is Earth Day.

"It fuels the fire," Lisa said about the case. "But I don't think it's enough."

JH Baxter violated environmental regulations for decades but was only charged based on actions committed between approximately Jan 2, 2019 and Oct 6, 2019. There are over 20 instances where three environmental agencies warned JH Baxter for going against air and water quality standards and hazardous waste regulations, according to a timeline from Oregon State University's research team.

It first started with headaches for newly wed Eric in August 2022.

"I felt like I was watching him die," Lisa said.

When doctors asked the couple if Eric was exposed to anything hazardous at work or his upbringing, a "lightbulb went off" for Lisa. Eric grew up right across the street from the site and went to Fairfield Elementary, which is less than a mile away from the site.

"My parents still live there," Eric said.

Two years later Eric is on a clinical drug and Lisa hopes JH Baxter is held accountable for personal injury.

Two class action lawsuits were filed in 2023, which includes "thousands of clients from the West Eugene area around JH Baxter, whose health or properties were allegedly affected by its operations," according to reporting from KLCC.

The truth behind JH Baxter

JH Baxter is a 205 acre wood treatment site in West Eugene, Oregon that produced wood products like railroad ties, utility poles and crossarms from 1937 till its closing in 2022.

Across from JH Baxter is the Bethel neighborhood where Eric was raised. The median household income is $42,500, over $20,000 less than the median household income for Eugene city. Hispanics are the largest minority population in the Bethel neighborhood at 19%.

A 2023 report from the Oregon Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) found that seven residential yards had soil “with dioxin concentrations over 40 parts per trillion (ppt).” This meant children under six years old in contact with bare soil for a year or longer were at high risk.

JH Baxter's recent case focussed on violating the Clean Air Act and Resource Conservation and Recovery Act. The court found that JH Baxter transferred wastewater to a wood treatment retort to "boil it off” between January 2, 2019, to October 6, 2019 and would send the remaining waste to an offsite for disposal. The retorts are cylindrical vessels where chemicals are forced into the wood to preserve the material and make it resistant to insects.

The wastewater would discharge into the air which meant the facility dismissed the Clean Air Act emissions standards for hazardous air pollutants, according to court records.

When the DEQ asked how many times JH Baxter used the retorts and requested for a documented record of the process, Baxter-Krause falsely wrote back that "[J.H.] Baxter does not have specific dates when this practice occurred.”

Yet the court found employees actually “maintained detailed daily production logs for each retort,” and the facility boiled off hazardous waste for 136 known days using multiple retorts.Baxter-Krause previously emailed only one retort was used.

The EPA has proposed JH Baxter to be on the Superfund's National Priority List.

How residents fought back

Students at Kalapuya High School, just under three miles away from JH Baxter, have come to classes with hives, rashes and asthma, said high school biology teacher Kelly Ferguson. One of her students was even diagnosed with cancer and had to miss an entire year of classes.

Nationally, almost 1800 schools are within just a mile away from a contaminated site on the Superfund's NPL list.

Using student self reported data and research from state nonprofit Beyond Toxics Waste, Ferguson teaches her students about environmental justice.

But with the Trump administration removing the environmental justice screening tool, Ferguson said it hasn't been easy to show how JH Baxter impacts the surrounding area.
